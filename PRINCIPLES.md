# Principles of the Remedial Legitimacy Framework

## What This Is

A plain-language summary of what the instruments in this framework consider prohibited, unacceptable, or triggering of remedial action. The formal instruments use precise legal and philosophical language. This document states the same positions simply.

---

## On Institutions and the People They Serve

1. **Institutions exist to serve people, not to perpetuate themselves.** An institution that has become primarily concerned with its own survival, funding, or authority — at the expense of the obligations it was created to fulfill — has lost the basis for the compliance it demands.

2. **Authority is conditional.** The obligation to comply with institutional authority depends on that institution performing its foundational duties. An institution that systematically violates the rights it is obligated to protect cannot simultaneously demand obedience from the people it is failing.

3. **Awareness creates responsibility.** An institution that is aware of harm being done to the people it serves and chooses not to act is participating in that harm. The same applies to individuals.

---

## What Is Prohibited

The following behaviors, when conducted or permitted by institutions against the people they are obligated to serve, are the conditions this framework exists to address:

### Violations of Person

- **Unlawful surveillance** — monitoring people without legal authority or legitimate purpose.
- **Arbitrary detention** — holding people without lawful basis.
- **Suppression of expression** — punishing, silencing, or retaliating against people for speaking, writing, organizing, or asking questions.
- **Retaliation** — making someone's life worse because they asserted their rights, sought accountability, or reported misconduct.

### Failures of Justice

- **Denial of justice** — refusing to hear, investigate, or remedy legitimate complaints.
- **Delay as denial** — allowing proceedings to take so long that the remedy becomes meaningless, while demanding immediate compliance from the people waiting.
- **Selective enforcement** — applying rules aggressively against some people and not at all against others, based on who they are rather than what they did.
- **Prosecutorial abandonment** — systematically declining to prosecute institutional misconduct while vigorously pursuing minor infractions by ordinary people.

### Institutional Betrayals

- **Saying one thing and doing another** — maintaining formal policies, mission statements, and public commitments that bear no relationship to actual institutional behavior.
- **Capture** — allowing an institution to be controlled by the interests it is supposed to regulate or oversee.
- **Weaponization of process** — using administrative procedures, legal mechanisms, or bureaucratic requirements as instruments of harassment or exhaustion rather than legitimate governance.
- **Destroying or suppressing evidence** — eliminating, concealing, or obstructing access to documentation of institutional misconduct.

### Degradation of Democratic Governance

- **Electoral manipulation** — undermining the integrity of elections through suppression, gerrymandering, or interference with the peaceful transfer of power.
- **Legislative capture** — allowing lawmaking to be determined by concentrated private interests rather than the public the legislature represents.
- **Erosion of judicial independence** — compromising the capacity of courts to function as a check on the other branches of government.
- **Exclusion by design** — structuring public processes, language, and procedures so that ordinary people cannot meaningfully participate in decisions that affect their lives.

### Targeting of Individuals

- **Ambient harassment** — the coordinated degradation of a specific person's professional standing, social relationships, living environment, or psychological stability through individually deniable actions that form a pattern only visible in aggregate.
- **Credibility destruction** — systematically creating conditions that make a person appear unreliable, unstable, or untrustworthy, so that their accurate account of what is being done to them will not be believed.
- **Professional interference** — using commercial, administrative, or informal channels to damage a person's career, reputation, or institutional access without attribution.
- **Social isolation** — deliberately disrupting a person's relationships, community connections, and support networks to reduce their capacity to function and to be corroborated.
- **Biography fracture** — creating years in a person's life history that they cannot explain in terms any institutional audience will credit, limiting their future access, opportunity, and credibility indefinitely.

### Manipulation of Populations

- **Covert preference engineering** — shaping people's political beliefs through invisible manipulation of their information environment, so that the beliefs feel self-generated but are externally produced.
- **Ambient democratic substitution** — preserving the formal mechanisms of democracy (voting, representation, deliberation) while the substantive content of those mechanisms is determined elsewhere, by actors whose identities and methods are invisible to participants.
- **Exploitation of susceptibility** — conducting influence operations against populations whose capacity for critical evaluation has already been systematically degraded by the commercial information environment.

## What Is Required

When these conditions are documented, the framework establishes that:

1. **Documentation is a duty.** Those who witness institutional failure have an obligation to record what they observe, preserve what they can, and make their testimony available.

2. **Silence is participation.** Remaining quiet about known institutional misconduct — particularly when one has the capacity to speak — is itself a form of complicity.

3. **Mutual aid is obligatory.** Signatories to the framework's instruments accept reciprocal obligations to support one another through evidence preservation, testimony, pattern correlation, and material assistance when institutions fail.

4. **Outside accountability is legitimate.** When domestic institutional mechanisms fail comprehensively, external accountability — through international mechanisms, distributed documentation networks, public advocacy, and diplomatic pressure — is not interference. It is the minimum non-complicit response.

5. **Confrontation may be warranted.** When documentation alone does not alter institutional behavior, active confrontation — through interposition, counter-documentation, non-cooperation, and delegitimation forcing — is philosophically justified as the exercise of obligations that institutional failure has activated.

---

## What Is Not Permitted

The framework also constrains its own participants:

- **No new harms.** Accountability actions must not create the conditions they claim to remedy.
- **No weaponization.** The framework's instruments cannot be used for personal vendettas, commercial advantage, or purposes unrelated to institutional accountability.
- **No conscription.** Participation is voluntary. No one may be pressured, guilted, or coerced into joining, and declining to participate may not be characterized as moral failure.
- **No fabrication.** Documentation must be accurate. Evidence must be authentic. False or manipulated records are grounds for exclusion.
- **No institutional capture.** The framework's own structures must not become the self-perpetuating bureaucracies it was designed to address.

---

## The Core Position

Institutions derive their authority from the obligations they fulfill. When an institution systematically fails those obligations — when it violates the rights it exists to protect, ignores the misconduct it exists to remedy, or serves the interests it exists to regulate — the people affected are not required to wait indefinitely for self-correction.

They are entitled to document, to witness, to organize, and to act.

That is what this framework is for.
