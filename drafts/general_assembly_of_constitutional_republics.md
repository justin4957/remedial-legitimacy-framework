STRATEGIC RATIONALE: WHY A GENERAL ASSEMBLY, NOT A CONFEDERATION
The history of international institutional design teaches a consistent lesson: organizations that demand deep sovereignty commitments before demonstrating practical value tend to either fail at the ratification stage or collapse under the weight of obligations that members resent but cannot renegotiate. Confederations, customs unions, and mutual defense pacts all require states to surrender meaningful authority on day one, betting that the benefits of cooperation will eventually justify the costs. When they don’t—or when the benefits accrue unevenly—the arrangement unravels.
The most durable international institutions have followed the opposite logic. They begin with a platform—a standing forum for consultation, norm development, and voluntary coordination—and allow deeper integration to emerge organically from demonstrated utility. The key insight is that the value of collective action is not abstract; it must be experienced before states will commit to it. A forum that produces one genuinely useful coordinated response to a shared crisis does more to build institutional loyalty than a treaty with fifty binding obligations that no one implements.
This is the logic behind the General Assembly of Constitutional Republics. Rather than asking states to delegate competences upfront, it creates a permanent institutional platform with five characteristics designed for rapid adoption and organic growth:
1. Near-Zero Sovereignty Cost of Entry
Joining the Assembly requires no delegation of authority, no binding policy commitments, and no alteration of domestic constitutional arrangements. A state accedes by affirming its commitment to constitutional republican governance and paying its assessed contribution. This radically lowers the barrier to participation, making it feasible for states that would never join a confederation or mutual defense pact to participate in a common forum.
2. Identity-Based Rather Than Interest-Based
Existing international organizations are generally organized around geography (AU, ASEAN, EU), security interests (NATO), economic integration (Mercosur, USMCA), or universal membership (UN). None is organized around the specific institutional characteristic of constitutional republican governance—a shared identity that cuts across geography, economic development levels, and security alignments. The Assembly fills this gap, creating a forum where Costa Rica and Germany, South Korea and Botswana, Uruguay and Estonia can engage as institutional peers sharing a common governance architecture.
3. Platform Logic Over Treaty Logic
Treaties create obligations. Platforms create opportunities. The Assembly is designed as a platform: it provides infrastructure for consultation, coordination, peer review, and voluntary joint action. States use the platform when it’s useful and ignore it when it isn’t. But the platform’s existence means that when a crisis or opportunity arises that requires coordinated constitutional-republican action, the institutional infrastructure is already in place—no ad hoc coalition-building required.
4. Graduated Depth Through Voluntary Instruments
The Assembly’s architectural innovation is the distinction between the standing compact (which all Members accept and which is deliberately lightweight) and voluntary instruments (protocols, joint declarations, coordinated action frameworks) that subsets of Members can adopt for deeper cooperation in specific domains. This means the Assembly can simultaneously serve as a low-commitment consultative forum for some members and a platform for intensive policy coordination for others—without either group constraining the other.
5. Built-In Pathway to Deeper Integration
If the Assembly proves its value over time, it can evolve into something more substantial through the protocol mechanism. A group of members that develops deep cooperation on, say, cybersecurity standards or democratic election monitoring can formalize that cooperation through a binding protocol without requiring all members to participate. Over decades, accumulating protocols could produce something that functions like a confederation in specific domains—but built bottom-up from demonstrated utility rather than top-down from sovereignty negotiations.

The critical distinction from the Community of Democracies is rigor. The Community’s broad, aspirational membership criteria have diluted its credibility—states with questionable democratic credentials have participated, undermining the organization’s ability to speak with authority on democratic governance. The Assembly addresses this through substantive eligibility criteria and a peer review mechanism that makes membership a meaningful credential rather than a participation trophy.



PART II: CHARTER OF THE GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF CONSTITUTIONAL REPUBLICS
Founding Compact
Preamble
The Peoples and Governments of the Founding Republics,
Affirming that constitutional republican governance—in which sovereignty resides in the people, authority is exercised through elected representatives, power is constrained by a written constitution, and fundamental rights are secured by an independent judiciary—represents a tested and resilient framework for legitimate self-government;
Recognizing that constitutional republics, whatever their geographic, cultural, or economic diversity, share structural commitments to the rule of law, separation of powers, peaceful transfer of authority, and the accountability of government to the governed that create a foundation for distinctive institutional solidarity;
Observing that the transboundary challenges of the present era demand coordinated responses, but that existing international organizations are not organized around the shared institutional identity that binds constitutional republics to one another;
Determined to create a permanent forum through which constitutional republics may consult, coordinate, and—where they choose—act collectively on matters of shared concern, without requiring the delegation of sovereign authority as a condition of participation;
Committed to an institutional design that is lightweight enough to attract broad participation, rigorous enough to maintain credibility, and flexible enough to accommodate deepening cooperation over time;
Have agreed as follows:


PART I — ESTABLISHMENT AND IDENTITY
Article 1 — Establishment
1. A General Assembly of Constitutional Republics (hereinafter “the Assembly”) is hereby established as a permanent international forum and institutional platform for sovereign states committed to constitutional republican governance.
2. The Assembly is not a sovereign entity, a federation, a confederation, or an alliance. It is a structured platform for consultation, coordination, peer review, and voluntary collective action among its Members.
3. Membership in the Assembly creates no delegation of sovereign authority, no binding policy obligations beyond the terms of this Charter, and no alteration of any Member’s constitutional order.
Article 2 — Purposes
1. The Assembly is established to:
(a) Provide a permanent forum for consultation among constitutional republics on matters of shared concern, including threats to constitutional governance, transboundary challenges, and opportunities for institutional cooperation;
(b) Develop, articulate, and promote shared norms and standards of constitutional republican governance through peer review, model instruments, and best-practice exchange;
(c) Facilitate voluntary coordination and, where Members choose, collective action on transboundary challenges that affect the capacity of constitutional republics to govern effectively;
(d) Serve as a platform through which subsets of Members may develop deeper cooperation through voluntary protocols, without requiring participation by all Members;
(e) Represent the collective voice of constitutional republics in international affairs on matters affecting constitutional governance, the rule of law, and democratic accountability;
(f) Provide mechanisms for the peaceful settlement of disputes among Members and for mutual support when constitutional governance is threatened.
Article 3 — Founding Principles
1. Sovereign Equality. Each Member Republic participates in the Assembly as a sovereign equal, regardless of size, population, military capacity, or economic power. Each Member has one vote.
2. Constitutional Identity. The Assembly recognizes and respects the diversity of constitutional traditions among its Members. It does not prescribe a single model of constitutional republicanism but affirms that all Members share core structural commitments: popular sovereignty, elected representation, constitutional supremacy, separation of powers, judicial independence, and protection of fundamental rights.
3. Voluntarism. Beyond the standing obligations of this Charter, all cooperation within the Assembly framework is voluntary. No Member may be compelled to participate in any protocol, coordinated action, or joint initiative.
4. Graduated Depth. The Assembly is designed to accommodate Members at different levels of engagement. The standing compact imposes minimal obligations; voluntary protocols enable deeper cooperation for those who choose it.
5. Transparency. All proceedings, decisions, reports, and financial statements of the Assembly shall be publicly accessible, with narrow exceptions for matters of security sensitivity approved by the Plenary.
6. Non-Interference. The Assembly and its organs shall not direct or compel any Member to alter its domestic constitutional order, legislation, or policy. Recommendations are advisory; compliance is voluntary unless a Member has assumed binding obligations through a specific protocol.


PART II — MEMBERSHIP
Article 4 — Eligibility
1. A sovereign state is eligible for membership in the Assembly if it demonstrates, through its constitutional order and actual practice, the following characteristics:
(a) A written constitution or equivalent fundamental law that serves as the supreme legal authority within the state’s territory;
(b) A republican form of government in which the head of state derives authority, directly or indirectly, from popular election rather than hereditary succession;
(c) Separation of governmental powers among distinct legislative, executive, and judicial branches, with meaningful checks and balances;
(d) An independent judiciary with the authority to review governmental action for constitutional compliance;
(e) Regular, free, and competitive elections conducted under conditions that permit genuine political contestation;
(f) Constitutional protection of fundamental rights and civil liberties, including freedom of expression, assembly, and association;
(g) Demonstrated commitment to the peaceful transfer of governmental authority.
2. Eligibility is assessed on the basis of institutional reality, not formal text alone. A state whose constitution meets the above criteria in form but whose governance systematically departs from them in practice does not qualify.
Design Note: These criteria are deliberately structural rather than ideological. They describe the institutional architecture of a constitutional republic without prescribing specific policy positions, economic models, or cultural arrangements. A social-democratic Nordic republic and a market-liberal Latin American republic can both qualify, as can states with parliamentary, semi-presidential, or presidential systems—provided the structural commitments are genuine.
Article 5 — Admission
1. A state seeking membership shall submit an application to the Secretariat, accompanied by documentation demonstrating satisfaction of the eligibility criteria.
2. The Credentials and Standards Committee (established under Article 9) shall conduct an assessment of the applicant’s constitutional order and governance practice, drawing on publicly available information, independent analyses, and consultation with the applicant.
3. Upon a favorable assessment, the Committee shall recommend admission to the Plenary, which shall decide by a two-thirds majority of Members present and voting.
4. Admission takes effect upon the applicant’s deposit of an instrument of accession and payment of its first annual contribution.
Article 6 — Membership Tiers
Design Note: The tiered membership structure is a deliberate innovation. Rather than a binary in/out that forces states to either fully commit or stay away, it creates multiple on-ramps. Observer status lets states participate without committing. Associate membership creates a pathway for states in democratic transition. Full membership signals verified constitutional republican governance. This structure maximizes the Assembly’s reach while preserving the credibility of full membership.
1. The Assembly shall recognize three categories of participation:
(a) Full Members: Sovereign states that satisfy the eligibility criteria of Article 4, have been admitted by the Plenary, and are current on their assessed contributions. Full Members have the right to vote, hold office, propose and participate in protocols, and access all Assembly services;
(b) Associate Members: Sovereign states that are committed to constitutional republican governance and are making demonstrable progress toward satisfying the eligibility criteria, but do not yet fully meet them. Associate Members may participate in Plenary deliberations, access Assembly advisory services, and join specific protocols open to Associates, but may not vote on substantive matters or hold senior office. Associate membership is granted by two-thirds Plenary vote upon recommendation of the Credentials Committee;
(c) Observers: Sovereign states, international organizations, or subnational entities with constitutional republican governance structures that wish to engage with the Assembly without formal membership. Observer status is granted by simple majority of the Plenary and carries no voting rights or financial obligations.
Article 7 — Peer Review and Continuing Eligibility
1. Every Member Republic shall undergo periodic Constitutional Governance Review on a cycle of not more than five years, conducted by the Credentials and Standards Committee with the participation of independent experts.
2. The Review shall assess the continued satisfaction of eligibility criteria in both constitutional text and governance practice, and shall produce a public report with findings and recommendations.
3. A Member found to have significantly departed from the eligibility criteria shall be subject to the following graduated response:
(a) Advisory Stage: The Committee issues recommendations and offers technical assistance;
(b) Watch List: If concerns persist, the Plenary may, by two-thirds vote, place the Member on a public Watch List, suspending its right to hold office and participate in new protocols;
(c) Suspension: If serious and sustained departure continues, the Plenary may, by three-quarters vote, suspend the Member’s voting rights and protocol participation;
(d) Expulsion: In extreme cases of fundamental abandonment of constitutional republican governance (such as a military coup, abolition of constitutional order, or systematic elimination of political opposition), the Plenary may, by three-quarters vote, expel the Member.
4. At every stage, the affected Member shall have the right to be heard, to present evidence, and to receive a reasoned decision. Restoration of full status shall follow demonstrated return to compliance.
Article 8 — Withdrawal
1. Any Member may withdraw from the Assembly by depositing written notice with the Secretariat. Withdrawal takes effect six months after deposit.
2. A withdrawing Member shall honor obligations under any protocols to which it is party, in accordance with the withdrawal provisions of those protocols.
3. A former Member may apply for readmission under the procedures of Article 5.


PART III — INSTITUTIONS
Article 9 — Organs
1. The Assembly shall have the following organs:
(a) The Plenary — the principal deliberative and decision-making body;
(b) The Steering Council — an executive committee for intersessional coordination;
(c) The Credentials and Standards Committee — responsible for membership assessment, peer review, and norm development;
(d) The Secretariat — the administrative and coordination body;
(e) The Advisory Panel of Jurists — providing independent legal and constitutional expertise;
(f) Such Standing Committees, Working Groups, and Protocol Bodies as the Plenary may establish.
Article 10 — The Plenary
1. The Plenary shall be composed of delegations from all Full Members, with each Member represented by up to three delegates designated by its government, at least one of whom shall be a member of the national parliament. Associate Members and Observers may attend and speak but not vote.
2. The Plenary shall convene in regular session at least once per year. Special sessions may be called by the Steering Council or at the request of one-third of Full Members.
3. Each Full Member shall have one vote. Decisions shall be taken as follows:
(a) Charter amendments and competence expansion: Three-quarters majority, plus ratification by two-thirds of Members;
(b) Membership decisions, suspension, and expulsion: Two-thirds or three-quarters majority as specified in Articles 5 and 7;
(c) Adoption of resolutions, recommendations, and model instruments: Simple majority;
(d) Procedural matters: Simple majority.
4. The Plenary shall:
(a) Deliberate on matters of concern to constitutional republics;
(b) Adopt resolutions, recommendations, and declarations;
(c) Approve the annual budget and contribution schedule;
(d) Authorize the opening of protocols;
(e) Elect the Steering Council and appoint the Secretary-General;
(f) Receive and act upon reports from all other organs.
Article 11 — The Steering Council
1. The Steering Council shall be composed of representatives from a geographically and constitutionally diverse subset of Full Members, elected by the Plenary for staggered terms of three years. The number of Council seats shall be determined by the Plenary, not to exceed one-quarter of Full Membership.
2. The Steering Council shall:
(a) Manage intersessional business and prepare the Plenary agenda;
(b) Coordinate the Assembly’s response to urgent matters between Plenary sessions;
(c) Oversee the Secretariat and budget execution;
(d) Represent the Assembly in its relations with other international organizations;
(e) Recommend the initiation of new protocols to the Plenary.
Article 12 — The Credentials and Standards Committee
1. The Committee shall be composed of independent experts in constitutional law, comparative governance, and human rights, nominated by Members and confirmed by the Plenary. Members shall serve in their personal capacity, not as representatives of their nominating state.
2. The Committee shall:
(a) Assess applications for membership against the eligibility criteria;
(b) Conduct periodic Constitutional Governance Reviews of all Members;
(c) Develop and maintain a body of Assembly Standards—non-binding guidelines articulating best practices in constitutional republican governance;
(d) Provide advisory opinions on questions of constitutional republican governance at the request of any Member or Assembly organ;
(e) Publish an annual State of Constitutional Governance report assessing global trends.
Article 13 — The Advisory Panel of Jurists
1. The Panel shall be composed of eminent jurists specializing in constitutional and international law, nominated by Members and confirmed by the Plenary, serving in their personal capacity for non-renewable terms of six years.
2. The Panel shall:
(a) Issue advisory opinions on the interpretation of this Charter at the request of any Assembly organ or Member;
(b) Provide legal analysis to support the development of protocols and model instruments;
(c) Advise on disputes among Members concerning the interpretation or application of this Charter or Assembly protocols;
(d) Serve as a resource for Members seeking constitutional reform advice.
3. Advisory opinions of the Panel are authoritative but non-binding. However, protocol bodies may designate the Panel as having binding interpretive authority within the scope of a specific protocol, by agreement of the protocol’s parties.
Article 14 — The Secretariat
1. The Secretariat shall be headed by a Secretary-General appointed by the Plenary on the recommendation of the Steering Council, serving a single term of five years.
2. The Secretary-General and Secretariat staff shall serve the Assembly in an exclusively international capacity and shall not seek or receive instructions from any Member government.
3. The Secretariat shall:
(a) Provide administrative and logistical support to all Assembly organs;
(b) Maintain records, archives, and the register of protocols;
(c) Facilitate communication among Members and between the Assembly and other international organizations;
(d) Prepare research, analysis, and reports as requested by Assembly organs;
(e) Manage the Assembly’s finances under the oversight of the Steering Council and Plenary.


PART IV — INSTRUMENTS OF COOPERATION
Article 15 — Spectrum of Assembly Action
Design Note: This article is the architectural core of the Assembly. It establishes a spectrum from lightweight, non-binding coordination to binding legal instruments, allowing Members to engage at whatever depth they choose. The protocol mechanism is what gives the Assembly its evolutionary capacity—the ability to deepen cooperation organically without requiring Charter amendments.
1. The Assembly may produce the following types of instruments, in ascending order of commitment:
(a) Resolutions: Non-binding expressions of the Plenary’s collective view on matters of concern. Adopted by simple majority;
(b) Recommendations: Non-binding guidance to Members on specific policy or governance questions, typically accompanied by model language or best-practice standards. Adopted by simple majority;
(c) Declarations: Solemn collective statements of principle or commitment, carrying heightened political and moral authority. Adopted by two-thirds majority;
(d) Coordinated Action Frameworks: Structured, time-limited programs of voluntary coordination among interested Members on specific transboundary challenges (e.g., coordinated election monitoring, joint cybersecurity exercises, shared pandemic preparedness protocols). Authorized by the Plenary by simple majority; participation is voluntary;
(e) Protocols: Supplementary instruments creating binding legal obligations among their signatories, within domains consistent with this Charter’s purposes. Protocols are the Assembly’s mechanism for deepening cooperation among willing Members without imposing obligations on others.
Article 16 — Protocols
1. Any Full Member or the Steering Council may propose the opening of a protocol. The Plenary shall authorize the opening of protocol negotiations by simple majority.
2. Protocols shall:
(a) Be open to all Full Members and, where the protocol so provides, to Associate Members;
(b) Specify their own governance arrangements, decision-making procedures, and dispute resolution mechanisms;
(c) Enter into force when ratified by a specified minimum number of Members (set by the protocol itself, but not fewer than three);
(d) Include provisions for amendment, review, and withdrawal;
(e) Be registered with the Secretariat and published.
3. Protocols may designate the Advisory Panel of Jurists as having binding interpretive authority within the protocol’s scope, or may establish their own dispute resolution mechanism.
4. No protocol may:
(a) Impose obligations on Members that have not ratified it;
(b) Conflict with the provisions or principles of this Charter;
(c) Purport to alter the constitutional order of any Member Republic.
5. Protocols create a pathway from voluntary coordination to structured cooperation. Examples of potential protocols include:
(a) Protocol on Mutual Election Monitoring: Establishing standing teams of observers from constitutional republics to monitor elections in fellow Members upon invitation;
(b) Protocol on Constitutional Resilience: Creating a rapid-response consultation mechanism when a Member faces threats to its constitutional order, including emergency advisory services and diplomatic support;
(c) Protocol on Digital Infrastructure Standards: Harmonizing cybersecurity, data protection, and digital rights standards among participating Members;
(d) Protocol on Judicial Exchange: Establishing a permanent framework for exchange programs, joint training, and advisory secondments among the judiciaries of participating Members;
(e) Protocol on Climate Cooperation: Coordinating transboundary environmental monitoring, shared scientific infrastructure, and joint mitigation commitments;
(f) Protocol on Anti-Corruption Standards: Establishing mutual transparency and accountability mechanisms, including coordinated asset disclosure requirements and mutual legal assistance.
Article 17 — Mutual Support for Constitutional Governance
1. When a Member’s constitutional republican order faces a serious threat—including but not limited to military coup, unconstitutional seizure of power, systematic undermining of judicial independence, or suspension of constitutional protections—any Member or the Steering Council may invoke the Constitutional Solidarity Procedure.
2. Upon invocation:
(a) The Steering Council shall convene an emergency session within 72 hours;
(b) The affected Member’s constitutionally legitimate authorities (or, if these have been displaced, their designated representatives) shall be invited to address the Assembly;
(c) The Assembly may, by two-thirds majority, issue a declaration of concern and offer mediation, advisory services, or other forms of support requested by the legitimate constitutional authorities;
(d) The Assembly may coordinate diplomatic responses among Members, including coordinated non-recognition of unconstitutional authorities, coordinated travel and financial restrictions, and joint representations in other international forums.
3. The Constitutional Solidarity Procedure is a framework for voluntary coordination, not collective enforcement. No Member is obligated to take any specific action, but the Assembly provides the platform for coordinated responses that would otherwise require ad hoc coalition-building.


PART V — FINANCE AND ADMINISTRATION
Article 18 — Finance
1. The Assembly shall be financed by annual assessed contributions from Full and Associate Members, calculated according to a transparent formula incorporating gross national income and capacity to pay, with a floor ensuring that no Member’s contribution is so small as to be negligible and a ceiling ensuring that no single Member bears more than fifteen percent of the total budget.
2. The contribution schedule shall be adopted by the Plenary by simple majority for a period of three years, renewable.
3. Protocol bodies shall be financed by their own parties, either through assessed protocol contributions or other arrangements specified in the protocol. Protocol costs shall not be borne by the general Assembly budget.
4. A Member that falls more than one year in arrears shall lose its right to vote in the Plenary until arrears are settled, except that the Plenary may, by simple majority, waive this consequence for a Member experiencing extraordinary economic hardship.
5. The Secretariat shall publish an annual financial statement, independently audited, detailing all revenues and expenditures.


PART VI — FINAL PROVISIONS
Article 19 — Amendment
1. Amendments to this Charter may be proposed by any Full Member or by the Steering Council.
2. Amendments shall require a three-quarters majority of the Plenary and ratification by at least two-thirds of Full Members in accordance with their constitutional procedures.
3. An amendment ratified by two-thirds of Members enters into force for all Members. A Member that cannot accept the amendment may withdraw under Article 8 without prejudice.
Article 20 — Relationship to Other International Obligations
1. This Charter shall be interpreted and applied consistently with the obligations of Members under the Charter of the United Nations, customary international law, and treaties to which they are party.
2. Membership in the Assembly is compatible with membership in any other international organization. Nothing in this Charter restricts a Member’s participation in other forums or arrangements.
3. The Assembly shall seek cooperative relationships with the United Nations, regional organizations, and other international bodies whose purposes are consistent with those of this Charter.
Article 21 — Dispute Resolution
1. Disputes among Members concerning the interpretation or application of this Charter shall first be addressed through direct consultation.
2. If consultation does not resolve the dispute, either party may request an advisory opinion from the Advisory Panel of Jurists, which shall issue its opinion within sixty days.
3. Members may, by mutual agreement, submit disputes to binding arbitration under rules established by the Panel.
Article 22 — Entry Into Force
1. This Charter shall be open for signature by states meeting the eligibility criteria of Article 4.
2. The Charter shall enter into force upon deposit of instruments of accession by not fewer than seven founding Members.
3. The original of this Charter shall be deposited with a Depositary designated by the founding Members, which shall provide certified copies to all Members and applicants.
4. This Charter is established in the official languages of the founding Members, each text being equally authentic.



PART III: IMPLEMENTATION ROADMAP
The following outlines a plausible pathway from concept to operational institution.
Phase 1: Convening (Year 0–1)
A founding coalition of five to ten constitutional republics from diverse regions convenes a Preparatory Conference to negotiate and finalize the Charter text. The coalition should deliberately span geographic, economic, and cultural diversity to establish the Assembly’s global character from the outset. Natural founding candidates include states with strong constitutional traditions and diplomatic capacity—without being dominated by any single regional bloc.
Key tasks: Finalize Charter text. Agree on initial contribution formula. Designate a Depositary. Establish the interim Secretariat. Begin outreach to prospective Members.
Phase 2: Launch (Year 1–2)
Upon achieving seven ratifications, the Charter enters into force. The inaugural Plenary session elects the Steering Council, appoints the Secretary-General, and adopts rules of procedure. The Credentials and Standards Committee is constituted and begins developing Assembly Standards and the peer review methodology.
Key tasks: Inaugural Plenary. Appoint Secretary-General. Constitute committees. Adopt first budget. Begin processing membership applications. Establish partnerships with existing international organizations.
Phase 3: Normative Development (Years 2–5)
The Assembly develops its normative infrastructure: Assembly Standards on constitutional governance, model instruments, and best-practice guidelines. The peer review cycle begins. Working Groups are established on priority themes (cybersecurity, election integrity, judicial independence, climate cooperation). The first Coordinated Action Frameworks are launched. Protocol negotiations begin in areas of strongest demand.
Key tasks: First round of peer reviews. Publication of inaugural State of Constitutional Governance report. First Coordinated Action Frameworks operational. Protocol negotiations underway.
Phase 4: Institutional Maturity (Years 5–10)
The first protocols enter into force, creating binding cooperation frameworks among willing subsets of Members. The Assembly’s normative voice gains international credibility. The peer review cycle produces its first meaningful consequences (Watch List placements, status changes). Associate membership provides a pathway for democratic transitions. The Assembly becomes a recognized interlocutor in international forums on questions of constitutional governance.
Phase 5: Evolution (Year 10+)
The Charter’s first scheduled review assesses institutional performance and recommends adaptations. Accumulating protocols create a progressively dense network of cooperation among willing Members. If the Assembly demonstrates sufficient value, some Members may propose a transition toward more structured confederal arrangements—but this emerges from demonstrated utility rather than being assumed at the outset.


CONCLUSION: THE INSTITUTIONAL GAP THIS FILLS
There are currently 193 member states of the United Nations, approximately 80–90 of which could plausibly be described as constitutional republics of some kind. These states share structural governance commitments that distinguish them from monarchies, one-party states, theocracies, and military governments—but they have no dedicated institutional forum through which to leverage this shared identity. They coordinate through geographic organizations that include non-republics, through security alliances that serve different purposes, through economic blocs that organize around trade rather than governance, or through the UN system that must accommodate every regime type.
The General Assembly of Constitutional Republics fills this gap. It is not a replacement for any existing institution. It is a new layer of institutional infrastructure—lightweight, voluntary, and designed for organic growth—that enables states sharing a specific governance architecture to consult, coordinate, develop shared standards, and act collectively when they choose to. Its value proposition is not the obligations it imposes but the opportunities it creates: for mutual learning, coordinated response to shared challenges, collective standard-setting, and a diplomatic identity rooted in constitutional republican governance rather than geography, military alignment, or economic interest.
The question is not whether such an institution would be useful—it plainly would be, as evidenced by the frequency with which democratic states create ad hoc coalitions to address challenges that a standing forum could handle more efficiently. The question is whether the institutional design is right: lightweight enough to attract participation, rigorous enough to maintain credibility, and flexible enough to deepen over time. This Charter is designed to answer yes to all three.
